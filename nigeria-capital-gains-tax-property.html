Nigeria Capital Gains Tax on Property

The old 10% flat Capital Gains Tax is gone. From January 1, 2026, gains on property are taxed under the unified Nigeria Tax Act 2025 at your normal income tax rate.

What changed

  • CGT Act repealed; gains now part of total taxable income
  • Individuals: gains taxed at progressive PIT bands (0% to 25%)
  • Companies: gains taxed at CIT rate (30%; 0% for small companies)

When CGT applies to property

  • Sale of land, buildings, flats, commercial property
  • Assignment of lease, surrender of tenancy rights
  • Indirect transfer – selling shares in a company that owns Nigerian property
  • Gifts to non-family (deemed disposal at market value)

How gain is computed

Gain = Disposal proceeds – (cost + improvements + legal fees) – Tax Written Down Value if capital allowances claimed

If you claimed capital allowances on the property, the Act requires using tax written-down value, not original cost – this increases the taxable gain.

Key exemptions (2026)

  • Principal Private Residence: gain on sale of your main home + up to 1 acre land, exempt once in lifetime
  • Reinvestment relief: full relief if proceeds from share disposal reinvested in Nigerian shares within same year (corporates)
  • Threshold for shares: disposal proceeds < ₦150m AND gain < ₦10m in 12 months = exempt
  • Government securities, approved pension funds – exempt

Example

You bought land in 2018 for ₦20m, sold in 2026 for ₦70m (no allowances). Gain = ₦50m. If your other 2026 income is ₦10m, total taxable = ₦60m – pushed into top bands (~25% marginal). Under old law you’d pay ₦5m (10%); now you may pay up to ₦12.5m.

Planning tips

  1. Sell in a low-income year to avoid band creep
  2. Keep proof of improvements, agency fees, perfection costs
  3. Diaspora owners: appoint a local representative; non-resident CGT applies on Nigerian-situs assets
  4. Use principal residence exemption wisely – once only
