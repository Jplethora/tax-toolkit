How to File Tax Returns in Nigeria (Step-by-Step Guide 2026)

Updated April 2026 8 min read

Filing your annual tax returns in Nigeria is a key part of financial responsibility. Whether you earn a salary, run a business, or freelance for international clients, submitting your returns to the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS) or your State Internal Revenue Service helps you stay compliant, avoid penalties, and build a verifiable financial history for loans, visas, and government contracts.

Who May Need to File

Not everyone files in the same way in Nigeria. Your filing obligation depends on how you earn income. While PAYE employees typically have tax deducted by their employer, you are still responsible for declaring all your income in an annual return.

  • Salary Earners (PAYE employees who have additional income) — If you earn rent, freelance income, dividends, or any side hustle income outside your salary, you must file a personal income tax return with your State IRS.
  • Self-Employed Professionals — Doctors, lawyers, accountants, architects, and other professionals practicing on their own must file annual returns and pay personal income tax via direct assessment.
  • Freelancers & Creators (remote workers, consultants, designers) — Income from Upwork, Fiverr, YouTube, or foreign clients is taxable in Nigeria once you are tax resident. You file with your State IRS.
  • Business Owners (sole proprietors, partnerships, SMEs) — You must file both personal income tax returns for yourself and, if registered as a company, annual Companies Income Tax returns with FIRS.

Information Typically Required

Before you start your filing on the FIRS TaxPro Max portal or your state's e-tax platform (like LIRS e-Tax, etc.), gather these documents to make the process smooth:

  • Tax Identification Number (TIN): Your personal or business TIN is mandatory for filing. You can get one free from FIRS or your State IRS.
  • Sources of Income: A complete list of everywhere you earned money during the year (January to December).
  • Business Profits (for self-employed): Your profit and loss statement showing turnover less allowable business expenses.
  • Employment Earnings (pay slips, Form H1): Your annual consolidated salary, allowances, and PAYE deductions from your employer.
  • Allowable Deductions: Proof of pension contributions, National Housing Fund (NHF), life insurance premiums, and other CRA-approved reliefs.

Record Keeping

Good records make filing faster and protect you during a tax audit. The FIRS and State IRS typically expect you to keep records for at least six years.

  • Income Records: All invoices, receipts, contracts, and payment confirmations for work done.
  • Expense Documentation: Receipts for business expenses like data, software, rent, transport, and marketing that are wholly, reasonably, and necessarily incurred.
  • Bank Statements: Personal and business account statements to reconcile your declared income.
  • Employment Summaries: Your annual pay summary, tax deduction cards, and pension statements.
