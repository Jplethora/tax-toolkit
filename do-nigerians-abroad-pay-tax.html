Do Nigerians Living Abroad Pay Tax in Nigeria?

Clear, practical 2026 guide for Nigerians in the UK, US, Canada, and worldwide.

Jerisma Plethora Tax Team

Licensed Nigerian Tax Practitioners

It’s one of the most common questions we receive at Jerisma Plethora: “I live in the UK/US/Canada — do I still need to pay tax back home in Nigeria?”

The short answer: It depends. Nigerian tax law does NOT automatically tax you just because you hold a Nigerian passport. Instead, it looks at two key things: your tax residency status and where your income comes from (source).

If you live abroad full-time, your foreign salary is generally not taxable in Nigeria. However, any income you earn FROM Nigeria — such as rent from a property in Lagos, profit from a business in Abuja, or dividends from Nigerian shares — is typically taxable in Nigeria, even as a non-resident.

1. The Nigerian Tax System and Residency Rules

Nigeria operates a residency-based tax system under the Personal Income Tax Act (PITA), as amended by the Finance Acts 2019–2023. Personal income tax is administered by State Internal Revenue Services (for individuals) and the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS) for specific categories.

You are considered a Nigerian tax resident if you spend 183 days or more in Nigeria in any 12-month period, or if you have a permanent home, employment, or significant economic ties in Nigeria. Tax residents are taxed on their worldwide income.

If you are a non-resident (living abroad for 183+ days with no full-time employment in Nigeria), you are generally only taxed on income that is sourced in Nigeria.

2. Income That May Still Be Taxable in Nigeria

Even if you live abroad permanently, the following Nigeria-sourced income is taxable:

Rental Income

Rent from any property located in Nigeria (Lekki, Abuja, PH, etc.) is taxable at progressive PIT rates (7%–24%).

Business Income

Profit from a trade, sole proprietorship, or partnership operating in Nigeria.

Employment Income (Nigerian Duties)

Salary for work physically performed in Nigeria, director fees from Nigerian companies.

Investment & Capital Gains

Dividends, interest, royalties from Nigerian companies, and gains on sale of Nigerian property or shares (10% CGT).

Most Nigerian banks and tenants are required to deduct Withholding Tax (WHT) at 10% on rent, dividends, and interest — this is not final tax, but a credit against your annual filing.

3. What About Your Foreign Income?

If you are a confirmed non-resident, your UK, US, or Canadian salary, self-employment income, or foreign investment gains are generally NOT taxable in Nigeria.

The major exception: if you are still deemed a Nigerian tax resident (e.g., you spend >183 days in Nigeria, maintain a full-time base, or are a government employee posted abroad), you must declare worldwide income in Nigeria — but you can claim a foreign tax credit for tax already paid abroad under a Double Taxation Agreement.

4. Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs)

Nigeria has active tax treaties with 14+ countries including the United Kingdom, Canada, France, Netherlands, South Africa, China, Spain, Italy, Belgium, and Pakistan. The US does not have a full DTA but offers unilateral relief.

These treaties prevent you from being taxed twice on the same income. To benefit, you typically need a Certificate of Tax Residence from your country of residence (e.g., HMRC in the UK, CRA in Canada) and file the appropriate claim with FIRS.

5. Compliance & Why Awareness Matters in 2026

Nigeria’s tax net is widening fast. With NIN-TIN-BVN integration, FIRS now easily tracks property transactions, rental receipts, and dividends to diaspora BVNs. You now need a Tax Identification Number (TIN) and tax clearance to:

  • Process a Certificate of Occupancy or Governor’s Consent
  • Renew contracts with government or multinationals
  • Open certain corporate bank accounts
  • Sell property above ₦10m

Voluntary compliance is cheaper than penalties, which can be up to 10% annual interest plus ₦50,000 for late filing.
